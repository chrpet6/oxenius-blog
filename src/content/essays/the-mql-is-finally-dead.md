The marketing-qualified lead had a good run.

For more than a decade, the MQL gave B2B marketing teams something wonderfully convenient: a number they could put on a dashboard.

A prospect downloaded a white paper.\
A prospect attended a webinar.\
A prospect clicked enough emails.\
A prospect hit a score threshold.\
A prospect became "qualified."

Marketing could celebrate. Sales could complain. Leadership could stare at the funnel chart and ask why pipeline was not moving faster.

This was always an awkward compromise. Everyone knew it. The MQL was never really a measure of buyer readiness. It was a measure of visible individual engagement. But it survived because it was easy to count, easy to report, and easy to connect to the old seller-controlled funnel.

AI is about to make that compromise impossible to defend.

The MQL is not dying because marketers suddenly admitted it was a bad metric. It is dying because the way B2B buyers make decisions no longer fits the unit of measurement.

A lead is a person.

A B2B purchase is a network.

And increasingly, that network is being shaped by AI before any individual fills out a form.

## The MQL measured the wrong thing

The basic flaw in the MQL is simple: it treats a buyer as an individual when most meaningful B2B purchases are collective decisions.

That was already true before AI. It is painfully obvious now.

Forrester's 2026 business buying research found that the typical buying decision now involves **13 internal stakeholders and nine external influencers**. Procurement professionals are decision-makers in **53%** of buying cycles. More than **60%** of buyers use trials to evaluate potential solutions, rising to **78%** for purchases of $10 million or more. ([Forrester][1])

That is not a lead. That is a small village with budget pressure, risk anxiety, competing priorities, political dynamics, and at least one person quietly trying to kill the project.

Yet many GTM teams still operate as if a single person's content engagement can tell them whether an account is ready to buy.

It can't.

A director downloading a report may mean the company is entering a serious evaluation. It may also mean the director is bored, writing a strategy memo, comparing vendors for a boss, preparing for a meeting, researching a competitor, or avoiding actual work on a Tuesday afternoon.

Meanwhile, the real buying motion may be happening elsewhere: in Slack threads, procurement workflows, board prep, analyst calls, private communities, peer conversations, product trials, and now AI-generated vendor comparisons.

The MQL sees the hand raise.

It misses the buying group.

## AI makes the old funnel look ridiculous

The old funnel assumed vendors could observe and influence most of the journey.

That assumption is breaking.

Gartner's March 2026 buyer research found that **67% of B2B buyers prefer a rep-free experience**, and **45% used AI during a recent purchase**. Gartner's point is not just that buyers dislike sales calls. It is that buyers are progressing through critical buying tasks more autonomously, using digital and AI-mediated paths before sellers enter the conversation. ([Gartner][2])

That matters because the MQL depends on visibility.

If the buyer reads your content, visits your website, fills out a form, and enters your nurture flow, you can score them. You can route them. You can declare them qualified.

But what happens when the buyer asks ChatGPT, Claude, Perplexity, Gemini, or Copilot to explain your category, compare vendors, draft requirements, summarize risk, prepare procurement questions, or write the internal business case?

Your CRM may see nothing.

Your marketing automation platform may see nothing.

Your lead score may see nothing.

And yet the buyer may have moved significantly closer to — or further away from — a decision.

This is the murder weapon.

AI does not merely automate marketing. It makes huge parts of buyer research invisible to the systems marketers have relied on to declare success.

## Forrester is already calling time on MQL obsession

This is not just a spicy operator take. The analyst world is starting to say the quiet part out loud.

Forrester's April 2026 "GTM Singularity" research says B2B firms are still clinging to old practices like impersonal mass emailing, gated content, siloed teams, and **marketing-qualified lead obsession**. Forrester argues that AI is making those practices untenable as buyer journeys change and more power shifts to buyers. ([Forrester][3])

That is a very polite way of saying the old GTM machine is cracking.

Forrester also argues that most GTM teams still rely on buyer engagement metrics that are no longer accurate indicators of performance in AI-driven buying environments. Instead, it says companies need to tie measurement more directly to customer goals. ([Forrester][3])

That should make every CMO uncomfortable.

Because the MQL is, at its core, a buyer engagement metric.

It tells you someone interacted with you. It does not tell you whether the buying group understands the problem, believes your solution, trusts your proof, has budget, can pass procurement, has executive support, or is aligned on urgency.

It tells you there was motion.

It does not tell you whether there was progress.

## The real metric is confidence

The future of GTM measurement is not "how many people engaged?"

It is "how much closer is this buying group to justified confidence?"

Gartner calls this "value clarity": the buyer's understanding of how a solution improves outcomes in their specific role and business context. Gartner says confident buyers are twice as likely to report a high-quality deal compared with buyers who have low decision confidence. ([Gartner][2])

That is the metric hiding underneath every real deal.

Can the champion explain the value internally?\
Can the CFO see the economic case?\
Can the CISO understand the risk?\
Can procurement justify the vendor choice?\
Can end users see why behavior needs to change?\
Can the executive sponsor defend the spend?\
Can the buying group agree on why now?

The MQL was never designed to answer those questions.

It was designed to answer a much narrower one: did someone engage enough for us to call them qualified?

That distinction was tolerable when buyers moved through more predictable, seller-visible journeys. In an AI-mediated buying environment, it becomes absurd.

## What replaces the MQL?

The replacement will not be one perfect metric. B2B companies love inventing acronyms, but the answer is not simply swapping MQL for another three-letter dashboard object.

The useful unit becomes something closer to **buying-group readiness** or **account confidence state**.

That means measuring whether an account is moving toward a decision, not whether a person crossed an arbitrary engagement threshold.

A serious GTM system should understand questions like:

Which stakeholders are involved?\
Which stakeholders are missing?\
Has the company validated the use case?\
Has the buyer consumed proof relevant to their industry, size, region, or maturity?\
Has the champion built an internal business case?\
Has procurement entered the process?\
Has the technical buyer tested the product?\
Has the CFO seen credible ROI logic?\
Has the security team reviewed the trust center?\
Has the trial produced evidence of value?\
Are AI tools describing our company accurately when buyers research the category?\
Are we helping the buying group reduce risk, or merely sending them more content?

That is a very different operating model.

In my own GTM thesis, the MQL gives way to account confidence state: a view of stakeholder coverage, proof consumption, trial progress, procurement movement, usage signals, and unresolved risk.

The old model asks, "Did this person look interested?"

The new model asks, "Can this account make a decision?"

## This will change marketing's job

The death of the MQL does not make marketing less important.

It makes weak marketing less defensible.

For years, too many B2B marketing teams were incentivized to generate activity that looked like demand: gated guides, webinar registrations, nurture clicks, inflated lead scores, and content syndication lists filled with people who had no real buying intent.

That game gets uglier in the AI era.

AI can generate infinite content. It can personalize emails. It can create campaign variants. It can summarize accounts. It can write follow-ups. It can produce more marketing activity than any team could have dreamed of five years ago.

But if the underlying strategy is weak, AI just produces bad GTM faster.

This matches the pattern I keep seeing across B2B tech companies: most teams are not short on tools, channels, or activity. They are short on clarity around positioning, offers, buyer understanding, and the systems that turn demand into revenue.

AI raises the penalty for that lack of clarity.

Vague positioning becomes vague AI output.\
Weak proof becomes weak buyer confidence.\
Generic content becomes invisible noise.\
Disconnected sales and marketing messages become trust killers.

Gartner's 2025 buyer research found that **69% of B2B buyers report inconsistencies between vendor websites and what sellers tell them**, and **73% actively avoid suppliers that send irrelevant outreach**. ([Gartner][4])

That is the future waiting for companies that respond to AI by producing more automated noise and calling it personalization.

## Sales should be relieved, not threatened

Sales teams have complained about MQL quality for years.

Often, they were right.

The classic argument went like this: marketing said, "We delivered 1,000 MQLs." Sales said, "These are not real opportunities." Marketing said, "You are not following up." Sales said, "They downloaded an ebook six months ago and do not know who we are."

A deeply stupid fight. Also a very common one.

The MQL created a local optimization problem. Marketing optimized for hand-raisers. Sales optimized for winnable deals. Leadership asked why conversion rates were broken.

AI gives companies a chance to end this argument.

Instead of debating whether a lead is "qualified," revenue teams should align around whether an account is advancing toward confidence.

That changes the handoff.

Marketing is no longer responsible for throwing names over the wall.\
Sales is no longer responsible for pretending every scored lead deserves pursuit.\
RevOps is no longer just reporting funnel leakage after the fact.

Together, the GTM team has to build a system that detects meaningful account movement, supplies the right proof, identifies missing stakeholders, routes human help where it matters, and learns from what actually moves deals forward.

That is harder than MQL reporting.

It is also much more useful.

## RevOps becomes the adult in the room

The MQL era let companies pretend that GTM was a clean sequence.

Marketing creates demand.\
SDRs qualify.\
AEs sell.\
CS retains.\
RevOps reports.

AI breaks that tidy chain.

Signals now come from everywhere: product usage, support tickets, sales calls, AI search visibility, website behavior, community discussions, customer proof, review sites, procurement activity, trial data, enrichment tools, renewal risk, and competitive mentions.

Someone has to turn that mess into an operating system.

That is why RevOps becomes more strategic in the post-MQL world. Not as CRM administration. As revenue architecture.

RevOps will need to define the new source of truth: what counts as real progress, which account signals matter, how buying-group readiness is scored, when humans should intervene, what AI agents can do, how data quality is governed, and how revenue teams measure outcomes instead of vanity engagement.

The companies that get this right will stop asking, "How many MQLs did marketing produce?"

They will ask, "Which accounts became more ready to buy, what changed, and what should we do next?"

## The CEO's dashboard needs to change

This is where the conversation gets uncomfortable.

The MQL has survived partly because executives like simple numbers. A board slide showing MQL growth looks clean. It suggests the revenue engine is working. It gives everyone something to point at.

But a clean number can still be a bad number.

A CEO should not want a bigger pile of leads if those leads do not represent real buying progress.

The better dashboard is messier, but more honest.

It should show whether target accounts are aware of the company, whether AI systems represent the company correctly, whether the right stakeholders are engaged, whether trials are converting into business cases, whether proof assets are being used, whether procurement risk is being reduced, whether sales cycles are compressing, and whether customer outcomes are being realized.

That is not as simple as counting MQLs.

But GTM is not simple anymore.

Maybe it never was.

## The MQL dies quietly inside the best companies first

Here is my prediction: companies will not all announce the death of the MQL at once.

Too many dashboards, compensation models, board templates, and marketing automation workflows depend on it.

Instead, the best companies will quietly demote it.

They may still track MQLs because legacy systems require it. They may still mention lead volume in meetings. But the real operating cadence will move elsewhere.

The serious conversations will be about account progression, buying-group coverage, proof gaps, trial evidence, AI visibility, stakeholder-specific value, and decision readiness.

The MQL will become what it always should have been: a weak signal, not the scoreboard.

And that shift will separate modern GTM teams from everyone else.

I have spent the last decade building and scaling GTM, marketing, growth, and product across B2B SaaS, fintech, and ecommerce — from Minna Technologies, which grew from a Swedish startup into a global SaaS fintech later acquired by Mastercard, to growth work across companies like Megadeals, Sysarb, and VC-backed portfolio companies. Across those contexts, the same lesson keeps showing up: activity is easy to create; buyer confidence is hard.

AI makes the easy part cheaper.

It makes the hard part more important.

## The death of the MQL is good news

The MQL deserves its funeral. But we should not mourn it too much.

It gave B2B teams a common language when the digital funnel was still young. It helped marketing prove some connection to revenue. It forced companies to operationalize demand. It was useful — until it became a substitute for thinking.

Now the market is moving on.

The next era of GTM will not reward companies for generating the most hand-raisers. It will reward companies that help complex, AI-augmented buying groups reach confidence faster than competitors.

That means better proof.\
Better positioning.\
Better buyer understanding.\
Better trials.\
Better internal business cases.\
Better human judgment at the moments where trust matters.

The MQL measured who looked interested.

The future measures whether the buying group can act.

That is a much higher bar.

And long overdue.
