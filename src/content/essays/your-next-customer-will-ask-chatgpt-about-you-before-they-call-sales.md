# Your Next Customer Will Ask ChatGPT About You Before They Call Sales

By Christoffer Oxenius

For years, B2B companies have treated the buyer journey like a funnel they could control.

Get the prospect to the website. Capture the lead. Send the nurture emails. Book the demo. Run discovery. Show the deck. Manage the stakeholders. Push the deal through procurement.

That world is not gone. But it is no longer the whole story.

The next version of B2B buying starts before your SDR reaches out, before your website session appears in analytics, and before your sales team even knows an account is in-market.

It starts when a buyer asks an AI tool a question like:

“Who are the best vendors for this problem?”

“Which of these platforms is best for a mid-market company in Europe?”

“What are the risks of implementing this?”

“Compare Vendor A, Vendor B, and Vendor C.”

“Write a business case I can send to my CFO.”

That invisible conversation may now be one of the most important sales conversations your company never gets invited to.

Gartner’s March 2026 B2B buyer research found that 67% of B2B buyers prefer a rep-free experience, and 45% used AI during a recent purchase. Gartner’s interpretation is blunt: buyers are progressing through critical buying tasks in more autonomous ways, and sellers can no longer rely on static collateral to influence those moments.

This is the future of go-to-market: not seller-controlled funnels, but AI-mediated buyer discovery.

And most companies are not ready.

## The first sales call is becoming the second opinion

In the old model, the first sales conversation was where the vendor helped define the problem, educate the buyer, explain the category, and frame the decision.

Increasingly, that framing happens elsewhere.

A buyer can now ask ChatGPT, Claude, Perplexity, Gemini, Microsoft Copilot, or another AI system to summarize a category, shortlist vendors, explain tradeoffs, identify implementation risks, generate procurement questions, and prepare internal stakeholders.

That does not mean the AI answer is always right. It often is not. It may be incomplete, outdated, or biased toward whatever information is easiest to find and parse.

But that is exactly the problem for vendors.

If AI systems misunderstand your positioning, omit your company from the shortlist, overstate a limitation, confuse you with a competitor, or summarize your category using someone else’s framing, you may lose influence before a human buyer ever lands on your website.

Forrester calls this the “visibility vacuum.” As B2B buyers shift research into answer engines, marketers lose visibility into the questions buyers ask, the information that shaped their thinking, and how their brand appeared relative to competitors. Forrester says buyers still visit provider websites to validate what they learned, but much of the earlier research happens off-site in AI systems that do not pass intent data back to vendors.

That is a big deal.

For the past two decades, B2B marketing has been built around visible signals: keyword searches, website visits, form fills, email opens, content downloads, demo requests, and intent data.

AI-mediated buying breaks that feedback loop.

The buyer may be doing deep research. The vendor may see nothing.

## Traditional SEO is not enough

Many companies will respond to this shift with a familiar playbook: “We need to optimize for AI search.”

That is partly true. But it is also dangerously incomplete.

This is not just an SEO problem. It is a clarity problem.

AI systems do not only need to find your company. They need to understand it correctly.

That means your public GTM layer has to become cleaner, sharper, and more structured. Your positioning needs to be obvious. Your category needs to be understandable. Your use cases need to be specific. Your pricing logic needs to be less mysterious. Your documentation needs to be readable. Your customer proof needs to be concrete. Your comparisons need to be honest. Your security and implementation answers need to be easy to extract.

Forrester’s latest GTM research argues that B2B leaders now need to design content for humans, buyer agents, and answer engines alike. It also says old practices like mass emailing, gated content, MQL obsession, and siloed teams are becoming untenable as AI hands more power to buyers.

This is where a lot of B2B companies will get exposed.

The issue is not that they lack content. Most have too much content.

The issue is that the content is vague, inconsistent, gated, outdated, internally focused, or scattered across too many disconnected surfaces.

The homepage says one thing. The sales deck says another. The help center says a third. The rep improvises a fourth. The case study makes a broad claim but does not prove it. The pricing page avoids the question. The security page is buried. The comparison page is marketing theater.

That was already bad. AI makes it worse.

Gartner’s 2025 buyer research found that 69% of B2B buyers report inconsistencies between vendor websites and what sellers tell them. The same research found that 73% actively avoid suppliers that send irrelevant outreach.

In a world where buyers and AI tools are triangulating information across sources, inconsistency is not a minor messaging issue. It is a trust problem.

## The buyer does not want more content. They want confidence.

The mistake many GTM teams will make is assuming AI-mediated discovery means they need to produce even more.

More blog posts. More comparison pages. More automated emails. More AI-generated thought leadership. More “ultimate guides.”

That is the wrong lesson.

AI makes production cheap. It does not make judgment cheap.

The companies that win will not be the ones that flood the internet with the most content. They will be the ones that create the most buyer confidence.

That distinction matters.

A buyer does not need another generic article explaining “what is revenue intelligence?” or “why customer onboarding matters.” They need answers to the questions that determine whether they can make a decision:

Will this work for a company like ours?

How long will implementation take?

What will our CFO care about?

What will our security team object to?

What happens if adoption is slow?

How does this compare with the vendor we already know?

What proof exists beyond handpicked testimonials?

What does this replace?

What does this not do?

Can I defend this decision internally?

Gartner calls this “value clarity”: a clear understanding of how a solution improves outcomes in the buyer’s specific role and business context. Gartner says confident buyers are twice as likely to report a high-quality deal as buyers with low decision confidence.

That should change how GTM teams think about content.

The goal is no longer simply to attract attention. It is to help a buying group reach justified confidence faster than they could with any other vendor.

## Your website is becoming a data product

The B2B website used to be a brochure. Then it became a conversion machine. Now it is becoming something stranger and more important: a data product for AI systems.

That sounds technical, but the practical implications are straightforward.

Your website, docs, pricing page, trust center, customer stories, comparison pages, product pages, and help center are no longer separate assets. They are all inputs into how humans and machines understand your company.

This means the future GTM website needs to answer questions more directly.

Not “drive operational excellence with an AI-powered platform.”

Say what you do.

Not “built for modern teams.”

Say which teams.

Not “seamless implementation.”

Show the steps, timeline, resources required, and common failure points.

Not “enterprise-grade security.”

Show the certifications, data handling policies, access controls, subprocessors, and risk documentation.

Not “proven ROI.”

Show the before-and-after numbers, customer segment, implementation context, and assumptions.

AI systems reward what is clear, structured, repeated, and corroborated. So do buyers.

The companies that understand this will treat their public information architecture as part of their revenue infrastructure. The companies that do not will wonder why they are absent from AI-generated shortlists.

## The new question every CEO should ask

When I look back at the companies I have helped scale — from early-stage startups to growth-stage SaaS — the pattern is rarely that teams lack activity. They have tools, campaigns, channels, dashboards, and meetings. What they often lack is clarity: clear positioning, clear buyer understanding, clear proof, and a clear system that turns demand into revenue.

AI does not remove that problem. It magnifies it.

If your positioning is vague, AI will generate vague summaries.

If your proof is weak, AI will surface weak evidence.

If your website is inconsistent, AI will amplify confusion.

If your category story is unclear, AI may borrow the story from a competitor.

This is why the most important GTM question is changing.

The old question was:

“Can buyers find us?”

The new question is:

“When an AI explains our category, are we included, understood, and trusted?”

That question should make a lot of leadership teams uncomfortable.

Because the answer is not solved by buying another sales engagement tool or asking marketing to produce more content. It requires a deeper rebuild of the GTM system.

It requires public positioning that is specific enough for humans and machines to understand. It requires proof that can survive scrutiny. It requires content that is ungated where buyers need it. It requires sales, marketing, RevOps, product, and customer success to stop contradicting one another. It requires companies to think less about lead capture and more about decision support.

In my own view of where GTM is heading, AI changes GTM from a seller-controlled funnel into a buyer-agent-mediated revenue system. The companies that win will not merely generate leads. They will help AI-augmented buying groups reach confidence, prove value, and act.

## The sales team still matters — just later, and differently

None of this means salespeople disappear.

In fact, the opposite may be true for complex deals.

As AI makes early research easier, human sellers become more important in the moments where trust, ambiguity, politics, and risk matter most.

Buyers may prefer rep-free discovery, but they still need help when they are determining fit, navigating tradeoffs, building consensus, and defending a decision internally. Gartner’s 2025 research found that while buyers often prefer self-service for general information, they prefer seller input for tasks requiring contextual intelligence, such as determining whether a product or service fits their company’s needs.

That means the seller’s job gets harder and more valuable.

The best reps will not be walking brochures. AI can do that.

They will be business-case builders, risk translators, stakeholder navigators, and trusted interpreters of messy context.

They will enter the conversation after the buyer has already formed a view. Their job will be to correct misunderstandings, sharpen the case, handle internal objections, and help the buyer make a decision they can defend.

That is a different skill set from pitching features.

## The winners will be the clearest companies, not the loudest

AI is going to flood B2B markets with more content, more outbound, more summaries, more comparisons, more automated follow-up, and more synthetic expertise.

That will make the average GTM motion noisier.

But it will also make the best GTM motions more valuable.

The winners will be the companies that are easiest to understand, easiest to trust, easiest to verify, and easiest to buy from. They will build websites that answer real buyer questions. They will publish proof that is specific enough to matter. They will make implementation, pricing, security, and limitations less opaque. They will design content not only for the champion, but for the CFO, CISO, procurement team, end user, and the AI tools assisting them.

The losers will do what B2B companies always do when a new channel appears.

They will produce more noise and call it strategy.

That will not work for long.

Your next customer may ask ChatGPT about you before they call sales.

The only question is whether ChatGPT gives them a reason to call.
